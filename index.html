I. Origin

What Is a Doubloon?

In the age of sail, a doubloon was a Spanish gold coin weighing 6.77 grams — the universal ransom, the universal wage, the universal measure of a pirate's worth. It crossed every ocean, changed every hand, and belonged to no nation.

In 2013, someone named shakezula decided that the blockchain needed its own doubloon. A Litecoin fork. Each block rewards exactly 6.77 DBL — the weight, cast in code. The total haul: eight million coins, or five years of sailing, whichever runs dry first.

No pretensions of being the next Bitcoin. No promises of wealth. Just the oldest agreement among free people: this coin is worth what we agree it is worth, and we answer to the sea, not to the shore.

II. The Code

The Pirate's Articles

Every ship that sails under the black flag does so by a compact. These are the articles by which Doubloons are governed — not by kings, not by banks, but by those who mine them.

I

No coin shall serve a master

A doubloon held in a bank is a sailor in chains. DBL exists on a distributed ledger — no central authority may freeze it, seize it, or inflate it away. Your keys, your coin. Your coin, your freedom.

II

The weight shall never change

The original doubloon weighed 6.77 grams. No more, no less. Every block mined returns exactly 6.77 DBL — not because a law demands it, but because the code is the law, and the code cannot be argued with.

III

Every hand earns an equal share of the sea

Pirate crews divided plunder by articles — each man's contribution was counted, each man's share was paid. Mining Doubloons is an open sea. No invitation required, no office to petition. Point your hash at the network and claim what you've earned.

IV

Trust the chain, not the man

A pirate's word was sealed in writing, signed before the crew. A Doubloon transaction is sealed in cryptography, witnessed by every node on the network. There is no appeal and no forgery. The ledger does not lie.

V

Spend freely, hoard not

Gold buried on an island feeds no crew. Doubloons in circulation build the network; Doubloons under a mattress rot with their owner. The value of a pirate currency is proved by spending it — on goods, on services, on the next voyage.

VI

The sea belongs to all who sail it

No nation owns the ocean. No corporation owns the protocol. DBL's source is open — fork it, improve it, run it, or abandon it. The right of every pirate is to sail, and the right of every coder is to run the node.

VII

Eight million is the horizon

All voyages end. When the eight millionth Doubloon is mined, the block reward ends — and what remains is the pure economy of those who kept the network alive. Scarcity is not a flaw. It is the entire point.

VIII

Ye are judged by what ye mine, not what ye claim

Any man can call himself a captain. Proof of work does not lie. Your hash rate is your reputation. Your nodes are your presence. On this network, as on the sea, presence is the only sovereignty that counts.

V. New Latitude

Same Lore, Another Sea

The 2013 Doubloons story was written for Scrypt miners and seed nodes. The spirit of it — no harbour master, rules baked into code, crew over crown — sits just as well on Solana: programs ship without asking a gatekeeper, state settles in the blink of an eye, and liquidity moves like trade winds.

Whether you honour the original chain or raise the flag on a new ledger, the articles do not change: your keys, your coin. Pirate currency does not need permission to exist — only a harbour bold enough to list it.
